# AU ETF thesis: a fund shelf beside the company library

**Date:** 2026-07-19
**Status:** draft scope, awaiting owner review. Prompted by the owner's question of 2026-07-19: what would it take to hold AU ETFs in the library with a proper thesis? Nothing here is pinned. The plans in CLAUDE.md's authority table stand untouched until a decision-log entry adopts this scope, and the staging below maps the work without committing it. Where it leans on direction, it leans on the steers recorded in the hedge fund gap plan of 2026-07-18 (single operator, own book, ASX first), which is itself a draft awaiting the owner's pin.

**Scope of the question:** ASX-quoted exchange-traded products (ETFs and quoted managed funds admitted under the AQUA rules), held in the library as first-class entities: a thesis, a small honest dashboard, and the same reproducibility discipline the companies get. Listed investment companies are out of scope by classification, not neglect: an LIC is a company with statutory accounts and already belongs to the company path. Funds on other markets are a later fork, like every other universe expansion.

## 1. Why, and why now

**The product dead-ends on ETFs today, in a different place on every path.** The ASX search index is built from the listed-companies file, which carries no ETPs, so an ASX fund code returns "No matches" (backend spec §8). A US fund trust that does file annual reports maps to almost nothing the EDGAR pipeline can anchor a fiscal year on, so ingestion deliberately never completes and the client's retry gives up against a persistent still-preparing answer (backend spec §5). Manual entry accepts anything, which is the trap: fund accounts cannot answer the operating-company line items, so the dashboard renders as typed not-meaningful cards, polite and useless, and the few numbers that would compute answer the wrong question entirely. An index fund's return on equity is the index's year, not a business's quality.

**The book will hold them.** The gap plan's direction makes this concrete: a fund of one runs thesis-before-position as a control, and the control only works if every holding can carry a thesis. A real own-money book on the ASX holds ETPs beside the concentrated names: an index core, a cash vehicle, perhaps a hedged international sleeve. Today those holdings would be the only positions with nowhere to hang written judgement, and the Gap 3 position join ("every position links to its entity's dashboard, thesis, and flag state") would have no entity to join to.

**It is cheap now.** The expensive parts already exist and generalise: the thesis machinery with its append-only, server-protected history; the sync protocol, which validates envelopes and treats payloads as opaque; the provenance grammar; the entry-form idiom; the design language. The marginal build is a record type, a small pure engine module, and screens that reuse the company idiom. What is deliberately absent stays absent: no model in the serving path, no new AWS services, no live data.

## 2. What this is not

- **Not price tracking.** Manual price entry stands (main plan §12.1); the fund shelf inherits whatever price posture the app has when it lands. No quotes, no charts of price.
- **Not performance or P&L.** Positions, returns, and attribution are the gap plan's Gap 3, a different surface. The shelf analyses vehicles; it does not measure the book.
- **Not advice.** Never buy/sell language, education framing, red-flag phrasing as questions to investigate: all of it carries over unchanged (main plan §15 and non-goal 2). The thesis records the owner's own reasoning.
- **Not screening.** ETPs stay outside the gap plan's Stage A universe machinery. This is a shelf in the library, not a screenable universe; nothing here feeds factors or backtests.
- **Not look-through.** The fund is the unit of analysis. Constituent-level analytics (weights, overlaps, factor exposures) are out of scope at every slice.
- **Not other markets.** AU only. US funds sit behind a different disclosure regime and different documents; they are a fork with their own scoping if ever wanted.

## 3. The entity decision: a fund is not a company with fewer fields

The wrong build is a `kind` flag on the company record. Fund data is facts and dated observations, not fiscal statements; every company surface (entry grid, dashboard, gates, engine) asserts operating-statement invariants a fund cannot meet, and a discriminator would thread conditionals through all of them. The company record today has no entity-type field at all, which is itself evidence: the shape never anticipated a second kind, and stretching it now buys nothing.

So: **a `fund` entity beside companies, sharing the id space and the machinery that is genuinely generic.**

- **Sync:** two new record types, `fund` and `fundFact`, joining the pinned set (`company`, `statement`, `price`, `thesis`, `flagDismissal`; api-contract sync schema). The server validates envelopes only and never reads payloads, so the server-side cost is the tuple widening and nothing else. The `thesis` record type is reused as-is: the server's append-only version protection keys on the record id and does not care what kind of entity the id names.
- **Client store:** Dexie moves to version 3 additively: a `funds` table and a `fundFacts` observations table. No existing table changes shape.
- **Export:** `funds` and `fundFacts` join the export file's `data` block. The pinned rule says additive fields never bump `formatVersion`; the build must confirm the current import parser tolerates unknown keys from a newer build before relying on that (two devices, one owner: a small risk, checked not assumed).
- **Theses:** the existing table and version machinery hold fund theses unchanged. The record's `companyId` field name widens in meaning to "entity id"; the data-model spec records the widened reading rather than paying for a rename migration.

## 4. The fund record

**Identity and structure (one row, mostly set once):** code, name, issuer, index tracked, asset class, domicile, replication basis (physical, sampled, synthetic), currency hedging, distribution frequency, DRP availability, inception date, currency. These are facts from the PDS, not judgements.

**Observations (dated rows, each carrying provenance: source document, its date, a page where one exists):**

| Observation | Value | Natural source |
|---|---|---|
| Fee record | Management fees and costs, basis points p.a., effective from | PDS (the headline figure under ASIC's fee disclosure regime, Regulatory Guide 97) |
| Fund size | FUM, integer cents at a declared scale, as-of date | ASX investment products monthly report, or the issuer page |
| Distribution | Cents per unit, period, franking percentage (optional) | Distribution announcements under the fund's code |
| Tracking record | Fund return and index return for a period, signed basis points | Issuer factsheet or annual report, page-referenced |
| NAV pair | NAV per unit and market price on the same day, integer cents | Issuer daily NAV page, plus the manual price idiom |
| Spread | Monthly average bid-ask spread, basis points | ASX investment products monthly report |
| Index record | Index name, as-of date | PDS or issuer announcement |

No fund financial statements are stored at any slice. A fund's annual report is fund accounts; the operating-statements grammar does not apply to it, and pretending otherwise is how the dashboard ends up lying politely. Money stays integer cents; rates and returns are integer basis points; formatting stays a separate final step. Everything is manual entry in slices 1 and 2, deliberately (§8).

## 5. Six cards and seven questions

The company dashboard's budget discipline applies, smaller: **the fund dashboard pins six cards**, and adding one means removing one. Codes F1 to F6 and FR1 to FR7 are defined by this document; code writes the identifiers.

| Code | Card | Identifier | Computed from | Not meaningful when |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| F1 | Total cost | `fundFees` | Latest fee record, % p.a. | No fee record |
| F2 | Tracking difference | `trackingDifference` | Three-year annualised when recorded, else the longest span recorded, labelled | No tracking record |
| F3 | Distribution yield | `distributionYield` | Trailing 12 months of per-unit distributions ÷ latest price | No price; distributions not covering 12 months |
| F4 | Premium/discount to NAV | `navPremiumDiscount` | (price − NAV) ÷ NAV from a same-day pair | No pair; pair dates differ |
| F5 | Fund size | `fundSize` | Latest FUM record | No FUM record |
| F6 | Average spread | `averageSpread` | Latest monthly figure | No spread record |

A facts panel (index, issuer, structure, hedging, inception, DRP) renders beside the cards and is not a card. Every card's detail sheet carries the formula, the exact inputs with their provenance, and an Owner's lens block, exactly as company cards do. The trust rule is unchanged and non-negotiable: a displayed number the owner cannot reproduce by hand from its sheet is a P0 bug.

**Items to investigate** (deterministic, explainable, phrased as questions; thresholds proposed here, pinned only by an owner review pass, as the company rules were):

| Code | Identifier | Fires when (proposed) |
|---|---|---|
| FR1 | `feeRise` | The latest fee record is above the previous one |
| FR2 | `wideTrackingGap` | The tracking gap runs beyond fees by more than 25 basis points over the three-year record |
| FR3 | `subScaleFund` | FUM below AUD 100 million after the third year since inception |
| FR4 | `shrinkingFund` | FUM down more than a quarter, year on year |
| FR5 | `persistentPremium` | Premium or discount beyond 50 basis points on the two most recent NAV pairs |
| FR6 | `wideSpread` | Monthly average spread above 40 basis points |
| FR7 | `indexSwitched` | The index record changed |

## 6. The thesis, which is the point

The machinery is reused verbatim: the one-draft-per-entity row, the append-only version table, the server-side version protection, the history sheet, the markdown export. What changes is the questions. The four stored section keys stay; the labels and prompts switch on entity kind:

| Stored key | The company asks | The fund asks |
|---|---|---|
| `business` | What does this business sell, who buys it, and why do they come back? | What job does this holding do in the book, and why is it passive rather than named positions? |
| `moat` | What keeps competitors from taking these economics? | Why this vehicle: which index, what methodology, what structure, hedged or not, and against which alternatives was it chosen? |
| `valuation` | What is the business worth, and what does the price assume? | What does it cost all-in, and does the tracking record show that the stated cost is the whole cost? |
| `kills` | What would have to be true for this thesis to be wrong? | What retires it: closure, an index or fee change, tracking rot, or the exposure case itself expiring? |

Reusing the stored keys is recorded debt, accepted with eyes open: the key `moat` holding vehicle-selection text is a wrinkle, but it keeps the schema, sync payloads, export shape, and version history untouched, and the alternative (per-kind section keys) costs an export-shape change and buys nothing the owner reads. Revisit only if a third entity kind ever appears.

Version snapshots adapt: where a company version can snapshot the financials that stood behind it, a fund version snapshots the facts panel and the card inputs as entered. The snapshot field is already optional, so the addition is additive.

## 7. Education: the Owner's lens, extended

One context block per card, in the same voice: why costs compound against the holder; why tracking difference is the truer fee; why premium and discount are an entry and exit cost, not a signal; why scale and spread are the liquidity facts that decide whether the vehicle is cheap to hold and cheap to leave. No investor is named (the naming decision, main plan §12.7, extends to fund copy); no issuer's marketing language is echoed; franking is recorded and displayed, never grossed up. After-tax arithmetic stays with the accountant, in the same spirit as the gap plan's records posture: record keeping, not advice.

## 8. Sources, and what is deliberately manual

Slices 1 and 2 are manual entry with provenance, full stop. That is not a compromise: it is the same posture as manual prices (main plan §12.1), it keeps every number page-referenced from a document the owner actually read, and the volumes are tiny (a book holds a handful of ETPs; a fee changes rarely; a NAV pair is two numbers a month if the owner cares that month).

Slice 3's automation candidates each get a validation gate before any code, the way the announcements-platform etiquette was validated live before the MAP client existed:

- **The ETP directory** (the ASX investment products list, published monthly): the search index addition, and a batch source for FUM and spread. Validate the file's shape, stability, and terms first.
- **Distribution announcements:** the MAP client already speaks to the announcements platform, and fund codes are codes; the resolver work is recognising distribution notices rather than statutory reports.
- **PDS and factsheet extraction:** the upload path (Journey E) exists; a fund-shaped prompt variant is a later, separately gated addition, not assumed.

The personal-use posture is unchanged (main plan §15): analysing public documents on the owner's own account, no redistribution.

## 12. What does not change

The credibility spine carries over whole: deterministic computation, typed impossibility instead of silent NaN, integer money, provenance to the page, append-only history for written judgement, the calm one-accent design language with semantic colour reserved for meaning, and the education-not-advice voice. A fund card that cannot be traced to a document the owner held is the same P0 bug a company card would be.
